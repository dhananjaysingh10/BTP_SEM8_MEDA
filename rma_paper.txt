Digital Microﬂuidic Biochip*

Abstract—A biochemical analysis is based on several labo-
ratory protocols that require repeated mixing of samples with
reagents. Sample preparation and analyte identiﬁcation steps
in such bioassays often involve mixing for solution preparation,
i.e., various ﬂuids are to be mixed in a certain volumetric ratio
in their resulting mixture. We present an efﬁcient approach
for automated mixing of three or more ﬂuids on a droplet-
based digital microﬂuidic biochip and design a layout for
implementing this algorithm. The proposed method reduces the
droplet transportation time from boundary reservoirs to on-
chip mixers as well as cross-contamination among overlapping
droplet routing paths. Simulation of several example solutions
reveals encouraging results.
I. INTRODUCTION
Digital microﬂuidic (DMF) biochips have now matured
into a potential technology for implementing biochemical
laboratory protocols (i.e., bioassays) on a single chip of a
few square centimeters in size [1]–[4]. Such portable lab-on-
a-chips (LoCs) have tremendous potential for point-of-care
clinical diagnostics, and the timely detection of cardiovascu-
lar diseases, cancer, diabetes, and HIV/AIDS crisis, on-site
biochemical analysis, analyte detection, DNA sequencing by
synthesis, and tissue engineering. For the control of DMF
biochips, automation for the user, and technology exploita-
tion, there is a pressing need for advances in computer-aided
design algorithms and techniques. A schematic diagram of
the top view of a DMF biochip is shown in Fig. 1(a) and the
cross-sectional view of a basic cell at a detection site of a
DMF biochip is shown in Fig. 1(b). Detailed descriptions of
DMF biochips and their applications can be found in [1], [2],
[5]. DMF-based LoCs use electrical actuation to manipulate
(transporting, merging, splitting, mixing, dispensing, etc.)
discrete nanoliter/picoliter volume of the sample or reagent
ﬂuids (called droplets) on a two-dimensional electrode array.
Droplet movement is achieved by a special phenomenon
called electrowetting-on-dielectric (EWOD) effect [5].
The basic idea of a microﬂuidic biochip is to integrate
multiple assay operations such as detection, sample pre-
treatment, and sample preparation on one chip. Front-end
functions for solution preparation — mixing of more than
two different reagent ﬂuids and dilution of a sample/reagent
with a buffer solution (i.e., mixing of two different ﬂuids)

In this paper, we present a layout-aware mixing algorithm
for automatic solution preparation of several sample/reagent
ﬂuids. The proposed algorithm uses an algebraic decompo-
sition approach that determines the mix-split steps required
to produce the solution with a target ratio. It produces a
sequence of reagent mixing steps (visualized as a “mixing
tree”), in which at each step two droplets of two different
ﬂuids are mixed both drawn from the reservoirs, or one from
a reservoir (or on-chip storage location) and the other already
residing at the mixing module. It has the following important
attributes:
• The proposed mixing algorithm can be used for au-
tomatic solution preparation on-chip with a desired
accuracy of the concentration factors of its constituents.
• The “mixing tree” has longer sub-sequences of mixing
steps with a small number of distinct ﬂuids (called
“dilution/mixing subtrees”); this property helps in al-
locating the reservoirs to the samples/reagents in the
proximity of the assigned mixers, which in turn reduces
droplet transportation time for moving them from the
reservoirs to the designated mixing modules.
• The proposed method leads to several disjoint “di-
lution/mixing subtrees” in the “mixing tree”; this
property aids in reducing the probability of cross-
contamination [11] among various droplet routing paths

II. AUTOMATIC SOLUTION PREPARATION
In many biochemical protocols, solution preparation is
a preprocessing step for mixing two or more ﬂuids in a
given ratio. Dilution of a biochemical sample is the special
case of solution preparation where it is mixed with the
buffer solution at a certain ratio corresponding to the desired
concentration factor. In this paper, we use the (1 : 1)-
mixing model, where two unit-volume (1V) ﬂuid droplets
are mixed together and after each mix operation, a split
operation splits the 2V-volume mixture equally into two 1V-
volume droplets. Next, one 1V-volume droplet is used in the
subsequent mixing step, while the other droplet is discarded
as a waste droplet. One mix operation followed by a split
operation is called a mix/split cycle.

internal (or non-leaf) node denotes a 1V droplet of the
mixture obtained after a (1 : 1)-mix/split step with two 1V
ﬂuid droplets corresponding to its two child nodes. A post-
order traversal of the mixing tree provides the sequence of
(1 : 1)-mix/split steps required to produce the target solution
(of n ﬂuids) denoted by the root of the tree. The total
number of internal nodes in a mixing tree, denoted by m,
is the total number of mix/split cycles required to obtain
the target solution from the supplies. The depth of a mixing
tree is denoted by d and the number of internal nodes at any
particular level is the number of mixing modules required
in the layout to complete all the mix/split operations of that
level in only one mix/split cycle by concurrently executing
them. Over all the d levels of the mixing tree, the maximum
number of internal nodes at any level is the degree of
parallelism (denoted by p) of the mixing tree. Hence, if this
mixing tree is implemented on the biochip, then p indicates
the minimum number of mixing modules required in the
chip layout to produce the target solution in only d mix/split
cycles. Otherwise, if there are fewer number of mixing
modules in the layout, then more than d mix/split cycles
are required for this solution preparation. A longest subtree
(with only one internal nodes at each level) of the mixing
tree is a dilution/mixing subtree with a limited number
(typically 2 to 4) of distinct leaf nodes (distinct ﬂuids).
In a mixing tree, there may exist multiple dilution/mixing
subtrees of different depths in different branches of the
mixing tree. The sum of depths of all the dilution/mixing
subtrees in a mixing tree is denoted by l. A dilution subtree
in a mixing tree represents mixing of only two ﬂuids at a
certain ratio. For the example mixing tree shown in Fig. 2(a)
the dilution subtrees are shown using shaded regions. For
this mixing tree m = 18, d = 7, p = 4 and l = 6.

C. Layout-Aware Solution Preparation
In practice, the reservoirs for supplying different sam-
ples/reagents and those for collecting waste or wash ﬂuids
are located at the periphery of a biochip. Consider a biochip
with two static mixing modules as shown in Fig. 3. The
mixing modules, M1 and M2, are three-electrode linear
array mixers of merge-and-split type (they can act in both
horizontal and vertical directions). As shown in ﬁgure, there
are seven reservoirs R1,...,R7 at the boundary of the ﬁxed
size 2D electrode array for loading the component ﬂuids and
two waste reservoirs for collecting waste droplets from the
chip during solution preparation. The arrow signs are used
to indicate some possible droplet transportation pathways to
and from the reservoirs and the mixing modules. Assume
that for each type of ﬂuid there is a dedicated reservoir
in the biochip. Then, two tasks are to be performed in
order to map a mixing tree onto a biochip: (a) one-to-one
mapping of distinct type of ﬂuids to the reservoirs (called as
reservoir allocation). (b) many-to-one mapping of internal
nodes to the on-chip mixers (called as mixer assignment).
If the number of internal nodes in a level of the mixing
tree (let p denotes the maximum value over all levels) is
greater than the number of available mixing modules, on-
chip storage units for intermediate droplets are needed to
complete the mixing process.

As stated earlier, the reagent ﬂuids are stored in the
reservoirs located at the periphery of the layout. Hence,
there is a linear ordering of the reagents whereas the mixers
in the layout have 2D requirement of reagents from the
reservoirs. If only a single mixer is used in the chip layout
for implementing any mixing tree with a degree of paral-
lelism more than one (p > 1), then the complete solution
preparation will take m number of mix/split cycles, m is the
total number of internal nodes in the mixing tree. However,
for p > 1, the solution preparation process can be made faster
by providing more mixers in the layout. Then, the two or
more mixers can be used to concurrently execute two or
more parallel mix/split steps of the mixing tree. Clearly, the
mixer assignment and reservoir allocation strongly depend
on the characteristics of the mixing tree. If the reagents
needed by different mixers are fewer in number, and the
intersection between the two sets of different reagents is
of small cardinality, reservoirs at the proximity of a mixer
can be allocated favorably for the corresponding reagents to
be mixed. Further, the chance of cross-contaminations [11]
among various droplet pathways also reduces.
For the above example ratio, in the mixing tree of Fig. 5,
the dilution subtrees are shown by the shaded regions. In
comparison to the BS method, the second method provides
a mixing tree with longer dilution subtrees (here l = 13,
whereas l = 6 for the BS method). This has motivated us to
ﬁnd an efﬁcient algorithm for automatic solution preparation
of several ﬂuids. The algorithm needs to provide a mixing
tree, for which the sequence of mix-split steps can be
efﬁciently mapped into a given DMF electrode layout with
a favorable placement of reservoirs on the boundary of the
biochip. Thus, the objective is to obtain a mixing tree with
longer dilution/mixing subtrees for a given target ratio.
III. LAYOUT-AWARE ALGORITHMS FOR SOLUTION

In (1 : 1)-mixing model, at each mixing step the concen-
tration factor of a sample/reagent in the mixture is obtained
by the arithmetic mean of concentration factors of two of
its constituents. Thus, the expression corresponding to the
concentration factor of target solution (denoted by the root
of the mixing tree) will have 2d in its denominator, where d
is a non-zero positive integer denoting depth of the mixing
tree. Therefore, the value of L is to be approximated as 2d,
for some d. For example, a target ratio 2 : 3 : 5 : 7 can be
approximated as 4 : 6 : 9 : 13 that can be used to obtain a
mixing tree of depth 5 and it can also be approximated as
120 : 181 : 301 : 422 so that its mixing tree is of depth 10.

V. CONCLUSION
In this paper, we have proposed an algorithm for auto-
matic solution preparation of several ﬂuids that can provide
a mixing tree with longer dilution/mixing subtrees with
fewer number of distinct leaf nodes. This reduces the droplet
transportation time from different reservoirs located at the
chip boundary to the on-chip mixers compared to the BS
method. Moreover, this method reduces the chance of cross-
contamination between droplet pathways of different ﬂuids,
which in turn reduces the cost and energy spent for the
management of wash droplets. However, the total number
of mix-split operations and hence the cost for solution
preparation may increase compared to the BS method. This
follows from the fact that the BS method produces a mixing
tree with a minimum number of leaf nodes for a given
precision of the concentration factors.
